Phonetics and Phonology

Phonetics
...
The study of speech sounds and how they are produced
  • Phonetic alphabet
    • Needed because many letters or combination of letters represent
      • The same sound
      • Same letter but multiple sounds
      • Combination of letters with single sound
      • No sound
    • E.g., ʌ - cup, luck
Phonology
...
Phonology is the study of how various sounds build patterns to form language units
  • Phoneme
    • The smallest unit of sound in a word
      • E.g., /k/
  • Homophones
    • Words that sound the same but spelled differently
      • E.g., knight and night
  • Allophone
    • Different ways to pronounce the same phoneme
    • E.g., /t/ in "hit, tip, and little"

Segmentation
...

Segmentation divides written text into units, such as words, sentences, paragraphs
Tokenization
...
  • Ambiguities
    • A character could have different meanings based on the context
    • Examples
      • Period
        • In most cases, final sentence punctuation symbol
        • Part of an abbreviation, e.g., the U.S.
        • Part of numbers, e.g., 1.5
      • Comma
        • In most cases, punctuation in a sentence
        • Part of numbers, e.g., 1,5
  • General approach (based on)
    • White space
    • Regular expression or rules
    • Machine learning, e.g., learn breakpoints
    • Character n-gram frequencies and size of input layer
  • Tools
    • Spacy, OpenNLP, neural tokenizer, etc.

Morphology
...

The study of the forms and formation of words

Words are made up of morphemes, e.g., construct can be divided into (prefix) con-, (base) struct, and (suffix) -ion.

Definitions
...
  • Bases are minimal free morphemes
    • E.g., cat
  • Affixes are bound morphemes
    • Suffixes
    • Prefixes
    • Infixes appear inside the base
      • E.g., fan + bloody + tastic
    • Circumfixes appear on both sides of the base
      • E.g., ge + sag + t
  • Decompounding
    • The process of splitting complex words into base words
      • E.g., Bücherregale -> Bücher + Regale
Morphological Normalization
...
The process of finding a standard version of words that are related in terms of their form
  • Stemming
    • Stripping off the endings of words
      • E.g., sitting -> sitt
    • General approaches
      • Two phases
        1. Desuffixing - removal of predefined word endings
        2. Recoding - transform the words obtained by 1) using defined rules
      • Porter's stemmer (two phases performed simultaneously)
    • Pros and cons
      • Not distinguishing between inflection and derivation
        • inflection - a change in the form of a word according to its grammatical function
        • derivation - creating a new word (derivative) from an existing word
      • Stems might not correspond to a real word form
      • Good for small text collections
  • Lemmatization
    • The process to undo the inflectional changes
      • E.g., cats -> cat
    • Usually combined with part-of-speech tagging for ambiguous cases
      • left -> leave (verb)
      • left -> left (adjective)
    • General approach
      • Simple solution
        • Have a list of all possible word forms and associated lemma info
      • Brute force
        • Try every possible segmentation of word and see which ones match known stems and affixes
      • Rule-based

Syntax
...

Syntactic ambiguity

Homographs are words that have the same spelling but different meanings, e.g., I saw the saw.

Why Syntax?
...
Syntax refers to the way words are arranged together
  • Infinite number of ways to arrange words to form sentences
  • Rules of syntax needed to specify possible arrangement of words
    • E.g., John saw Mary with a telescope
      • John saw | Mary with a telescope
      • John saw Mary | with a telescope
Definitions
...
  • Constituents
    • Phrases that can be identified by passing one of the tests
      • Substitution
        • Can be replaced by a pronoun or another noun phrase
      • Movement
        • Can be moved to another position in the sentence
      • Coordination
        • Can be joined with another phrase using a conjunction
      • Question
        • Can be used to answer a wh-question
    • E.g., The dog ate a cookie
      • The dog ate it (substitution)
      • A cookie was eaten by the dog (movement)
      • The dog ate a cookie and a sausage (coordination)
      • What did the dog eat? A cookie (question)
  • Head
    • The word that determines the syntactic type of the phrase
    • E.g., the large house (noun phrase)
  • Modifiers
    • Words that add info about other words
    • Premodifiers (before the head)
      • E.g., all flights from Cleveland
    • Postmodifiers (after the head)
      • E.g., all flights from Cleveland
  • Semantics
    • The study of the meaning of words, phrases, sentences, or documents
  • Lexical semantics
    • The study of the meaning of words

⭐ Syntactic Ambiguity
...
  • Arises when one sentence has more than one syntactic structure
  • Attachment ambiguity
    • A constituent can be added to different paces of the parse tree
    • E.g., I shot an elephant in my pajamas
      • VP -> VP PP I (shot an elephant) (in my pajamas)
      • NP -> NP PP I shot (an elephant) (in my pajamas)
  • Coordination ambiguity
    • Scope of the conjunction undetermined
    • E.g., Black cats and dogs like to play
      • (Black cats and dogs) like to play
      • (Black cats) and (dogs like to play)
  • Garden path sentences
    • Sentences that lead you along a path suddenly turn out not to work
    • E.g., The horse raced past the barn fell

Pragmatics and Discourse
...

  • Pragmatics
    • The study of how context influences the interpretation of meaning
    • E.g., Two people are in a room, and one says, "It's really cold in here."
      • Literal interpretation
        • A simple statement about the temperature
      • Pragmatic interpretation
        • The speaker might be indirectly asking for the window to be closed
  • Discourse
    • The study of how to create coherent group of sentences
    • Coherent example
      • I was very hungry. I made a sandwich.
    • Incoherent example
      • I was very hungry. The car needs fuel.
